Introduction

Ultimaker 3D printers are mostly used for prototyping models on the carpet floor like fab labs or design offices. Now, Ultimaker is expanding its business area to the concrete floor like production lines in factories. In this project, we are redesigning the 3D printers managing system for factory manufacture.

My task:In this project, I was mainly responsible for (1) conducting desktop resarch, (2) concluding design goal and deisgn requirments, (3) building up user interface with Adobe XD, (4) doing quatitiative analysis of user test results, (5) editting video for showcase.

Research

In the initial research, we did desktop research and interviews to (1)build up the persona of the targeted group, as well as (2)analyze the existing problems of the current Ultimaker software system.

User Research

In this project, we aim to design a managing software system for 3D printer operators working in manufacturer factories. The challenge of the user research is that we cannot really get access to the real 3d printer operator, at least in the Netherlands. Because this occupation might not exist yet. Thus, we conducted semi-structured interviews with 3d printer technologists or makers in the university or design company, and did desktop research by watching videos of some existing 3d printing factories outside the Netherlands on Youtube. Then, we got some useful insights about how the 3d printer operators work.



Mass management. We defined our task as designing a managing software system for 3d printer operators working in factories with a large amount (about 10-40) of 3d printers which are used for manufacturing purposes.



Job scope. The working task of 3d operators is making a printer fleet run smoothly and efficiently. Their job includes: (1)assigning printing jobs (3d models) to printers, (2) doing physical maintenance on 3d printers, (3)taking out finished 3d models, (4) and switching printing material filaments. Therefore, the scope of this project is contingent on the working tasks of the target group..



Mobility of work. Usually, their working behavior requires a high level of mobility. Because they are also in charge of other production machines such as CNC millers besides 3d printers. Thus, they need to move around the whole factory floor.



We use a persona to sketch an overview of our target group [persona], and also, conclude that the new system should:
[1] Allow mobility of operators in the workplace
[2] Allow high level of control of multiple 3d printers.



Product Analysis

We analyse the Ultimaker ecosystem including four interfaces: (1) digital tab on Ultimaker 3d printer, (2) Cura (for processing 3d models) (3)Cura Connect (for managing printing jobs), (4) Ultimaker app (for remotely monitoring printing jobs). They are all relevant products relevant to the printing process, developed by Ultimaker. We analysed the product by inviting 3d printing expert and novices to walk through one complete process of printing a model.

Insight1. The overlaying product ecosystem structure
Cura, Cura Connect, Ultimaker printers and Ultimaker app are all relevant products relevant to printing process, developed by Ultimaker. They share similar names, different 'locations' (on the user's computer, the Ultimakers themselves and the user's phone, respectively) and overlapping functionalities. This easily cause confusion for user to choose the right tool for a specific task. Ideally, this distinction does not matter to the user, to make sure the whole process of printing job is a fluent process.



Insight2. Insufficient depth of feedback
Ultimaker has its own desktop printing management software: Cura Connect. Also, they develop Ultimaker app for same function. However, this products over-simplify the printing process and give too few details and notifications when problems occur. Ideally, the products provide enough information for people to solve problems and take over control without having to consult the manual or online resources.

Insight3. Unfriendly interface for a large quantities of printers
The current print job management interface is currently designed for only a small amount of printers. With more than about ten printers, it becomes harder to find and manage the right printer and print jobs, because the interface can only contain 2 or 3 task blocks. Ideally, the user can find the printer immediately.

Design exploration - Concept divergence

In this phase, we explore the design ideas by generating two design concepts, and perform a comparative study. The aim of this exploration is to conclude the requirements and insights for the final design decisions. We generate 2 concepts which mostly differ in ways of giving the operators a feeling of control. We quickly build up the interface with Balsamiq and test with a paper prototype.

Select one sub-context for agile validation
In order to efficiently develop the concept and gain more insight, we choose one essential part of the whole printing process and develop a prototype. This part is "selecting and grouping available 3D printers". Which are proposed to be an important part of 3d printer job management based on expert interview.

Actually the conclusion is a little bit cliche to some extent, maybe you can imagine that even without the tests. But, it still helps us to figure out the direction of the next step: create a feeling of control by combining the INFORMATION OVERVIEW and WORK ON THE GO.

Essential Design Decision

From the previous research and concept comparative tests, we made up 3 final decisions for the design concept.

[1] Tablet Application

A tablet application is preferred for integrating an information overview (it has a bigger screen) with a high level of mobility (it is portable). Also, a significant amount of companies already use company tablets in the operation of factory machines. Therefore, it is a reasonable medium to base our design on. Then, we try to integrate the functionality of the existing desktop app and mobile app.

Thus, a new product is launched, we call it: "Cura flow". Also, this decision led to a new simplified ecosystem overview.



[2] Schedule-based Interface

Keeping track of which printer group is printing which model in what status, is one of the main tasks of the future operator. Therefore, we decided to make the schedule the main screen of the application. To make sure the users recognize it as a schedule, similar use cues as existing calendar and scheduling applications were used. Then we draft a structure of the schedule, an X-Y axis coordination system, the x-axis presents the time while the y-axis shows the printer. It indicates information needed to be able to identify a job: (1) which printer, (2) how many printers,(3) how much time, are devoted to (4) which printing job.



[3] Color-code task list

Additionally, keeping track of everything that needs to be done is an important requirement of operators. Hence, a clear tasklist with color coding is added. The color-code aims to help operators quickly identify the type of task on which printer.

Usability Test

In the usability test, To test the usability and qualities of our concept, we went to a few companies to test our prototype with operators in the real context of a factory workshop. In this text, we raised following research questions:

[1] Which interaction element impede people from finishing task? How serious is it?
[2] Which interaction element should be priorited for better experience?
[3] Do the people feel like the product matches the context they work in?

Method

This time, digital prototypes and cardboard 3D printers are utilized in this test. Participants were shown a 3d printing video to get a more enriched experience, and were then asked to perform three tasks: (1) create a new production line (2) take out finished model (3) fix error of printer (clogged nozzle).

Qualitative Analysis

For qualitative data, we collected relevant quotes from the audio recordings. We cluster them based on its relevance between each other and figure out more deep and abstract meaning behind the quotes. Then the quotes were divided into 3 categories. What we are more focused on is the 10 usability problems.

Quantitative Analysis

For quantified analysis, we focus on error rate data. We checked the prototype screen recordings for usage patterns and collected all the error interactions in every step of each task.

1. Data collection about
usage pattern while testing
2. Collect errors on interface
3. Categorize the errors and
count times of try for the task completion

These errors were categorized into four levels: 'first try', 'second try', 'third try or more', and 'guidance needed to finish task'. We made an overview of the error occurrence of every single step of the three tasks. Since participants of the four tests differentiated a lot in knowledge about 3D printers and digital interface, we found that the test results were non-consistent. But we still managed to conclude useful insights.

Qualitative Analysis Result, Error occurance in each task


Result - combination of qualitative & quantative results

Finally, we prioritized the 10 usability problems based on the severity and frequency and put them in a graph. With severity, we mean the level of the negative impact the problem has on being able to successfully finish a task. With frequency, we mean the number of times the user comes across the task in their daily work. Based on the frequency-severity graph, we made an ordered list of the usability problems that need solving.
